# The Conjugal Dashboard

## When relational excellence becomes a trap

You've succeeded at your relationship.

That's precisely the problem.

---

Weekly check-ins have been in your shared calendar for three years. The rules of nonviolent communication are posted on the fridge. You took the Enneagram test together during a training weekend, and since then, you know exactly how the other functions: their defense mechanisms, hidden needs, comfort zones. You even know each other's love language — acts of service for him, quality time for her — and you make sure to speak it.

You've even invested in preventive couples therapy. Not because things are going badly, but precisely because they're going well and you want to maintain this trajectory. Why wait for the crisis?

You manage your relationship like you manage your career: with clear objectives, implicit performance indicators, and remarkable adaptability. You don't fight, or barely. When tension appears, you address it quickly: you talk about it, identify the unmet need, find a compromise. Important decisions are made by consensus. Roles are clarified — who manages what, who takes care of what. Your children's education follows a coherent strategy you co-constructed after reading several books on conscious parenting.

Your friends envy you. Some ask for advice. You've become, without really seeking it, that model couple who "figured something out" that others haven't grasped yet.

And yet.

There's this diffuse sensation, almost indescribable. It's not acute suffering. It's not even latent conflict. It's more of an **absence**. A flatness. As if you'd built together a perfect machine — well-oiled, silent, efficient — but over time, you'd forgotten why it existed.

Desire didn't extinguish in drama. It didn't collapse after betrayal or violent argument. It simply... evaporated. Progressively. Without identifiable reason. Without culprit.

You still touch each other, sometimes. But it's mechanical. Programmed. Like an appointment you honor because it's in the calendar. You make love on Saturday nights, after putting the kids to bed, and it's fine. Technically fine. But there's nothing that pulses anymore. Nothing that surprises. Nothing that overflows.

You still talk, of course. But about what? Logistics, mainly. Who picks up the kids. What menu for Thursday. What decision to make about vacation. Even your "real" conversations — the ones where you take time to listen to each other — have something... formal about them. As if you were applying a protocol. "How do you feel?" "What did you experience today?" You ask the right questions. You give the right answers. But there's no more surprise. No more unpredictability. No more tension.

You know him too well. Or rather, you **believe** you know him completely. You know what he'll say before he says it. You anticipate his reactions. You manage his moods. There's nothing left to discover.

And he, probably, thinks the same thing about you.

So you remain there, side by side, in this impeccably organized life, with this chilling realization that imposes itself slowly, insidiously, without you daring to formulate it clearly.

---

> _"Everything functions, but nothing lives."_

---

## Your Relationship Has Become a Spreadsheet

What most high-performing couples don't see is that they treat their relationship like a **spreadsheet**.

If I input A (clear communication) + B (material stability), I must necessarily get C (conjugal fulfillment). It's mathematical. It's rational. It's the formula of the project-couple, the business-couple, the strategic partnership-couple.

You've applied to your intimate life the same principles that made you succeed professionally: clarity of objectives, division of tasks, process optimization, continuous improvement. And it works. On paper, it works perfectly.

This approach rests on three invisible presuppositions, so deeply anchored that you've probably never questioned them.

### 1. "If something's wrong, there's a solution."

Something's wrong? There's friction? A misunderstanding? Then you must **identify the bug** and fix it. Read a book. Take a workshop. Consult a therapist. Learn a new communication technique. Adjust. Optimize.

The underlying idea is that the relationship, like a machine, can be **perfected**. That with the right tools, the right knowledge, the right practices, you can eliminate all dysfunctions and reach an optimal, stable, lasting state.

### 2. "The other must be predictable."

In the project-couple, the other isn't really a mystery to explore, an unknown continent to discover. They're a **partner** with whom you share common objectives: the house, the kids, retirement, perhaps a certain social status or lifestyle.

Their behavior must be predictable. Manageable. Optimizable. You don't want surprises. You want coherence. Reliability. Each person must play their role efficiently, respect their commitments, contribute to the common project.

Love, in this framework, strangely resembles a form of **contractual loyalty**. You don't ask yourself "Do I desire them?" but rather "Are they doing what they should be doing?"

### 3. "Desire can be worked on."

If desire drops, there's necessarily an **identifiable cause**: not enough time together, too much work stress, lack of attention, routine setting in, accumulated fatigue. And if the cause is identifiable, then it's **fixable**.

You just need to add ingredients: romantic vacations, more physical exercise, a date night once a week, surprises, novelty. You can restart desire like you restart an engine. It's a question of **will** and **means**.

The idea that desire might **not** respond to this logic — that it might be fundamentally **elusive, irrational, unmanageable** — is intolerable. Because it would mean you don't have control. And you've spent your life cultivating control.

---

## The Invisible Framework That Traps You

Except that **a relationship is not a machine**.

It doesn't respond to input-output logic. It can't be decomposed into independent variables. It doesn't stabilize through optimization.

A living relationship is a **complex system**, nonlinear, unpredictable, crossed by tensions, contradictions, shadow zones. It isn't managed. It's **lived**. It isn't mastered. It's **traversed**.

And here's the terrible paradox you're discovering:

---

> _"The more you optimize, the more you clarify, the more you standardize, the more you kill precisely what makes a couple alive."_

---

Alterity. Mystery. Creative tension. The unexpected. Fear. Risk. Raw vulnerability, unfiltered by a communication technique.

You've built a system so efficient, so **secure**, that you've eliminated any possibility of surprise, destabilization, reinvention. You've transformed the other into predictable data. And you yourself have become predictable data for them.

The fundamental error isn't in the execution. You're doing everything right. You're an exemplary couple.

The error is in the **conceptual framework itself**.

You've applied to intimacy a logic that works for managing projects, businesses, technical systems. But a relationship isn't a project. It's not something you **accomplish**. It's something that **happens** — or doesn't.

And as long as you remain in this framework, as long as you seek to **solve** what cannot be solved, as long as you try to **control** what cannot be controlled, you'll only reinforce the problem.

---

## What You Don't See Yet

You're probably looking for a solution right now.

"What could we do differently?"

"What tool are we missing?"

"What technique haven't we tried yet?"

It's your reflex. It's what has always allowed you to progress. Identify the problem, find the solution, apply, adjust.

But this reflex is precisely what keeps you stuck.

Because what's collapsing isn't an isolated element of your couple that you could repair. What's collapsing is the **very structure** on which you built it.

You can't patch up an obsolete structure by adding new layers. You can't save the project-couple by becoming even more efficient, even more conscious, even more equipped.

What it would take is accepting to **let this model die**. Accepting that what worked for years — this impeccable management, this perfect harmony, this absence of conflict — is precisely what's strangling you today.

But accepting that means accepting to **no longer know** what you are together. It means accepting to lose control. It means accepting that the relationship could fail, even if you do everything "right".

And for now, you're probably not ready for that.

---

## The Uncomfortable Lucidity

So you remain in this uncomfortable lucidity.

You know something's wrong. You know it's not a question of effort. You know the tools you're using no longer work.

But you don't yet know **what else to do**.

And that's exactly where you need to be.

In this zone of discomfort.

Without immediate solution.

Without method to apply.

Just this reality imposing itself, slowly, inexorably:

**The perfect couple you've built is killing you slowly.**

Not because you built it badly.

But because **perfection itself is the problem**.

---

## The Question That Remains Suspended

How long will you keep pretending everything's fine?

How long will you keep optimizing, adjusting, compensating, while something essential continues to extinguish?

How long will you keep searching for the right technique, the right tool, the right method, when **the entire structure** must be questioned?

These questions don't call for immediate answers.

They don't demand action.

They simply ask that you **stay** with them.

That you accept not knowing.

That you tolerate this zone of uncertainty, discomfort, non-mastery.

Because it's only there — in this void, in this silence, in this absence of solution — that something new can emerge.

Maybe.
